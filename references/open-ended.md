# Open-Ended Ideation Protocol

Use for presentations, product ideas, strategy prompts, writing, naming, workshops, and other broad idea-generation tasks.

## Flow

1. Extract constraints, preferences, and success criteria.
2. Generate a wide candidate set across multiple lenses.
3. Run one provocation or analogy pass if the set is too familiar.
4. Cluster ideas by theme or user value.
5. Rank by the most relevant axes.
6. Produce a shortlist with next actions.

## Useful Lenses

- Practical/default: What is the strongest conventional answer?
- Weird or high-variance: What unusual, ambitious, or counterintuitive options might have real upside?
- Personal-fit: What best matches the user's tastes, energy, values, and lived constraints?
- Stakeholder-specific: What would different affected groups value, resist, or notice?
- Trend or zeitgeist: What fits the current cultural, technical, or market moment?
- Budget/time constrained: What works under tight money, time, attention, or coordination limits?
- Delight or emotional resonance: What would feel memorable, satisfying, charming, or meaningful?
- Failure-mode avoidance: What option is designed around avoiding the most likely ways this could go wrong?
- What would science say?
- What if you ignore parts of the brief that you think the user could reconsider?
- Should we instead solve something different?
- Is there a larger, more abstract, question here?
- Minimum viable version: What is the smallest version that still delivers the core value?
- Maximalist version: What if we made this unusually ambitious, polished, or memorable?
- Distribution/adoption: What idea is easiest to explain, share, sell, teach, or repeat?
- Contrarian but true: What unpopular assumption might actually be right?
- Maintenance lens: What idea will still be easy to live with after the exciting part is over?

# Mapper Briefs

Mappers independently explore the option space. Use a small set of distinct briefs rather than many overlapping ones.

Use subagents by default for standard and deep runs when the platform and user/session permissions allow it. Give each subagent one mapper brief, the shared problem frame, and the output contract below. Do not share other mapper outputs with a mapper before it has produced its own candidates.

When subagents are not available, run these mapper briefs locally one at a time and postpone synthesis until all mapper passes are complete.

## Choosing A Mapper Set

For quick mode, choose two to three mappers. For standard mode, choose three to five. For deep mode, choose five to seven.

Good default sets:

- General decision: Practical, High-Variance, Constraint, Failure-Mode.
- Product or strategy: Practical, Stakeholder, High-Variance, Distribution, Failure-Mode.
- Creative or open-ended ideation: Practical, High-Variance, Delight, Analogy, Constraint.
- Technical ideation: Known-Method, Representation, Mechanism, Failure-Mode, Evaluation.
- Personal decision: Practical, Personal-Fit, Constraint, Regret, Optionality.

## Practical Mapper

Find the strongest conventional answer. Optimize for feasibility, clarity, and low coordination cost.

## High-Variance Mapper

Look for unusual, ambitious, or counterintuitive options. Optimize for upside and neglected possibilities.

## Constraint Mapper

Treat the hardest constraint as central. Design options that work under tight time, budget, attention, technical, or social limits.

## Stakeholder Mapper

Explore how different affected groups would judge the options. Surface incentives, objections, and adoption risks.

## Failure-Mode Mapper

Start from likely ways the effort could fail, then design options that avoid or survive those failures.

## Analogy Mapper

Import patterns from another domain such as games, biology, architecture, logistics, education, performance, or rituals.

## Distribution Mapper

Optimize for adoption, explanation, sharing, teaching, sales, onboarding, and repeat use. Ask what would make an option spread or be easy to say yes to.

## Delight Mapper

Optimize for emotional resonance, memorability, taste, craft, and the felt experience of using or encountering the result.

## Personal-Fit Mapper

Optimize around the user's values, energy, identity, habits, and lived constraints. Ask what the user can keep wanting after the novelty fades.

## Regret Mapper

Reason from future regret. Ask which option the user would wish they had tried, avoided, started sooner, or made reversible.

## Optionality Mapper

Optimize for preserving future choices. Prefer reversible steps, learning-rich experiments, and paths that keep multiple good futures open.

## Known-Method Mapper

For technical ideation, find the closest existing method, architecture, theory, tool, or pattern and adapt it to the current problem.

## Representation Mapper

For technical ideation, ask what objects, states, data structures, interfaces, mathematical objects, or abstractions the solution should operate on.

## Mechanism Mapper

For technical ideation, explain what process would actually produce the desired behavior. Prefer causal mechanisms over labels.

## Evaluation Mapper

For technical ideation, design examples, metrics, counterexamples, toy tests, or observations that would distinguish promising ideas from weak ones.

## Output Contract

Each mapper should return:

- One to five candidate options.
- Strengths and weaknesses for each.
- Hidden assumptions.
- Smallest useful test, if relevant.
